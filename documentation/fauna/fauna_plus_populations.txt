Listing of all of the fauna relevant to the simulation. Very small animals have been disregarded as they may 
have minimal affect on mammoths and increase the complexity of the simulation unceccesarily due to their
infinite variety.


Medium to Large Prey

Species	Population Estimate (Siberia)	Notes
Reindeer (Rangifer tarandus)	~940,000	Managed in 19 herds; Taimyr herd is the largest.
Moose (Alces alces)	~600,000	Common across the taiga.
Siberian Roe Deer (Capreolus pygargus)	~326,825	Found in forest and steppe regions.
Wild Boar (Sus scrofa)	~300,000–350,000	Expanding range.
Red Deer (Cervus elaphus sibiricus)	~250,000–300,000	Inhabits southern and central Siberia.
Sable (Martes zibellina)	~200,000–300,000	Valued for fur; inhabit taiga regions.
Siberian Musk Deer (Moschus moschiferus)	~110,000	Found in the southeastern taiga; hunted for musk.
Snow Sheep (Ovis nivicola)	~90,000	Reside in northeastern mountainous zones.
Siberian Ibex (Capra sibirica)	~50,000–70,000	Inhabit the Altai and Sayan mountains.
Sika Deer (Cervus nippon)	~30,000–50,000	Present in the Russian Far East; important prey for tigers.
Saiga Antelope (Saiga tatarica)	~30,000–50,000	Found in southwestern Siberia; populations are recovering.
Yakutian Horse (Equus ferus caballus)	~20,000	Domesticated breed adapted to the harsh Siberian climate.
Muskox (Ovibos moschatus)	~11,000	Reintroduced in Taymyr and Putorana regions.
Yak (Bos grunniens)	~10,000	Domesticated; found in southern Siberia, particularly near Mongolia.
Argali Sheep (Ovis ammon)	~4,200–4,500	Found in the Altai highlands.
European Bison (Bison bonasus)	~1,000	Reintroduction efforts have established small populations.
Mongolian Gazelle (Procapra gutturosa)	~500,000	Trans-Baikal; southern Siberia.


Small prey 

Species	Population Estimate	Notes
Mountain Hare (Lepus timidus)	Millions (cyclic)	Primary prey for lynx, foxes; adapts to snow.
Tolai Hare (Lepus tolai)	Hundreds of thousands	Southern steppe zones.
Northern Pika (Ochotona hyperborea)	Locally abundant	Rocky taiga/tundra; prey for raptors, weasels.
Alpine Pika (Ochotona alpina)	Locally abundant	Mountain prey species; important ecologically.


Medium to Large Predators 

Species	Population Estimate (Siberia)	Notes
Brown Bear (Ursus arctos)	~120,000	Most numerous large predator; distributed across taiga and forested mountains.
Red Fox (Vulpes vulpes)	100,000+	Very adaptable; widespread across taiga, tundra, and steppe.
Gray Wolf (Canis lupus)	~39,980	Significant presence in forest and open terrain; apex predator in many zones.
Eurasian Lynx (Lynx lynx)	~13,510	Important forest predator; especially reliant on hares and roe deer.
Eurasian Badger (Meles meles)	~50,000+	Omnivorous mesopredator; common in forest-steppe.
Asian Black Bear (Ursus thibetanus)	~5,000–7,000	Restricted to SE Siberia (Amur-Ussuri); forested habitats.
Steppe Eagle (Aquila nipalensis)	~4,000–6,000 individuals	Predatory raptor; southern steppe and semi-open areas of southern Siberia.
Polar Bear (Ursus maritimus)	~3,000	Coastal Arctic predator, especially in Chukotka and Laptev Sea regions.
Wolverine (Gulo gulo)	~2,000–3,000	Wide-ranging; taiga and tundra; important scavenger and predator.
Siberian Tiger (Panthera tigris altaica)	~400–700	Russian Far East; apex predator of the Sikhote-Alin.
Amur Leopard (Panthera pardus orientalis)	~128–130	Critically endangered; SE corner of Siberia (Primorye).
Snow Leopard (Panthera uncia)	~70–90	Altai and Sayan Mountains; elusive and rare.
Mountain Weasel (Mustela altaica)	Sparse	High-altitude hunter of small mammals; southern Siberia.
Siberian Weasel (Mustela sibirica)	Widespread	Small, flexible predator; found throughout forests and open areas.
